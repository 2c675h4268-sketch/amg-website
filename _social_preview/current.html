Space  ·  Weekly Analysis

What this week means for where space is headed.

What Happened

After a June 12 IPO that briefly pushed SpaceX past Amazon and made Musk the first trillionaire, shares reversed, shedding roughly $600 billion and settling near $156. Musk's net worth fell to about $957 billion.

Separately, SpaceX added billions in debt while cutting interest costs, tested its Starfall cargo reentry vehicle, and Firefly neared a $110 million EXIM loan.

Why It Matters

The post-IPO whipsaw exposes how much of SpaceX's valuation rested on AI-adjacent enthusiasm rather than launch and Starlink fundamentals, punishing late buyers while validating early insiders who sold into the peak. Public markets now discipline a company that spent two decades avoiding their scrutiny, and the debt refinancing shows management racing to lock in cheap capital before sentiment sours further.

Expect the sector to bifurcate: proven cash generators hold, speculative space names deflate.

Watch: Next 90 Days

SpaceX first post-IPO earnings print: The initial quarterly disclosure will reveal whether Starlink cash flow justifies the valuation or confirms the correction has further to run.
Starfall reentry test result: A successful orbital cargo delivery demonstration would open a new commercial logistics revenue line and reframe SpaceX beyond launch and connectivity.
Firefly EXIM loan board vote: Approval of the $110 million loan would confirm Washington is subsidizing a second-tier US manufacturer to counter foreign competition, pressuring rivals lacking government backing.

What this week means for where the grid is headed.

What Happened

Nvidia-backed Firmus will build a 360MW campus in Batam, Indonesia, with access to 170,000 chips through 2028 and up to $30 billion in offtake. South Korea is negotiating with Samsung and SK Hynix to pull fab construction forward to 2034-2035.

Abu Dhabi's MGX is weighing a multi-billion acquisition of DayOne, and Foxconn beat on AI rack demand.

Why It Matters

Nvidia is no longer just selling chips; it is seeding demand by financing the buildouts that consume them, locking sovereign wealth and national industrial policy into its supply chain. That vertical pull favors memory suppliers like Micron and assemblers like Foxconn while leaving developers exposed to the one thing capital cannot buy quickly: local power and community consent, as Florida's opposition shows.

The constraint is shifting from silicon to siting, grid, and political tolerance.
