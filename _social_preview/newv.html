Artificial Intelligence  ·  Weekly Analysis

What this week means for where AI is headed.

What Happened

Apple filed suit accusing OpenAI of stealing confidential hardware information, prompting a public X clash between Sam Altman and Elon Musk. Meanwhile, OpenAI safety systems head Johannes Heidecke departed, and Instagram's Adam Mosseri confirmed the platform will charge users for AI access.

Why It Matters

The era of free, subsidized AI features is ending: Instagram charging users and Meta yanking its AI remix tool two days after launch show that compute costs and user backlash are forcing platforms to price and retreat. OpenAI faces a legal, personnel, and reputational squeeze precisely as Apple positions itself as the disciplined hardware incumbent.

Watch: Next 90 Days

Apple v. OpenAI hardware suit progression: Any injunction or discovery ruling would expose how far OpenAI's consumer hardware ambitions have advanced and constrain its device roadmap.
OpenAI safety leadership vacancy: Whether a credible successor to Heidecke is named will show if alignment governance is being rebuilt or quietly downgraded amid commercial pressure.
Instagram AI pricing rollout terms: The specific price points and adoption will test whether consumers pay directly for generative features or whether paid AI stalls at scale.

What this week means for where space is headed.

What Happened

SpaceX, now public and added to the Nasdaq-100, hit a record low despite a consensus $237 price target implying 65% upside. NASA chief Jared Isaacman said the US is "very much in a space race" with China and is helping Blue Origin probe a rocket explosion narrowed to a possible engine fault.

Why It Matters

Blue Origin's stumble hands SpaceX pricing power on heavy launch, while Isaacman's China framing converts Artemis from science into a defense budget line that survives political turnover.

Watch: Next 90 Days

SpaceX first public earnings print: The debut report will test whether Starlink and launch revenue justify the $237 target or confirm the market's skepticism at record lows.
Blue Origin explosion root-cause finding: Confirmation of an engine fault and a pad rebuild timeline determines how long SpaceX holds an uncontested heavy-lift monopoly.
Artemis III lunar landing schedule: Any date slip against China's stated crewed timeline would validate or undercut Isaacman's urgency and reshape NASA funding priorities.

Longevity  ·  Weekly Analysis

What this week means for where longevity is headed.

What Happened

Apnimed, a late-stage sleep apnea drug maker, filed for a US IPO on Friday, and ARPA-H awarded $43 million to Philadelphia gene therapy researchers including Jim Wilson and the Baby KJ team. Separately, the FDA approved Sanofi's Sarclisa Escena, the first subcutaneous multiple myeloma treatment via a hands-free on-body injector.

Why It Matters

The IPO filing plus ARPA-H's bespoke gene therapy push confirm capital and federal money are flowing again toward rare-disease and durable-cure platforms, favoring established operators like Wilson over speculative startups. Meanwhile Sanofi's wearable injector shows the near-term money is in reformulating proven drugs for convenience, a lower-risk path as blockbuster patents expire and AI-driven funds like Yosemite hunt those openings.

Watch: Next 90 Days

Apnimed IPO pricing: The deal's valuation and aftermarket performance will show whether the biotech IPO window is genuinely open or just cracked for late-stage assets.
ARPA-H bespoke gene therapy milestones: Early data from the Wilson and Baby KJ programs would prove whether individualized rare-disease therapies can scale beyond one-off proofs of concept.
Sanofi Sarclisa Escena uptake: Adoption of the on-body injector will test whether convenience reformulations can defend revenue against looming patent cliffs and biosimilars.

Energy & Compute  ·  Weekly Analysis

What this week means for where the grid is headed.

What Happened

Arista Networks rose nearly 32% in Q2 2026 and raised annual revenue guidance on demand for CPU-driven networking, while Tower Semiconductor shipped its five millionth coherent photonic IC for Marvell's optical business. Nvidia supplier Hon Hai beat sales on continued AI rack demand.

Why It Matters

The money is quietly migrating from the GPU itself to what connects and feeds it: networking, optical interconnects, and power. Nvidia still anchors demand, but Arista, Marvell, Tower, and Hon Hai capture margin as hyperscalers diversify and scale, meaning the durable returns increasingly sit with suppliers who make giant GPU clusters actually usable.

Watch: Next 90 Days

Nvidia's Computex integrated platform: Whether the June 2026 hardware-plus-software-plus-models bundle tightens Nvidia's lock-in or accelerates customer efforts to route around it.
Apple M7 Ultra with 1.5TB memory: A credible Blackwell alternative for inference would validate large unified-memory silicon as a real path away from GPU scarcity.
Texas data center power capacity approvals: Gigawatt-scale generation and interconnect decisions will determine whether AI compute buildout is throttled by grid limits, not chips.
